02 Flight

Alicia May Thomas was born at 7:35 in the morning on the 18th of November, as the first and only ochild of Amanda Winter and Ramon Thomas, who at that time had been married for a little over a year. As a child, she had been highly adventurous, making friends with the kids in her neighbourhood and every creature within a mile's radius of her house; in school, this translated into a natural curiosity that enabled her to easily outperform her peers, much to the surprise of her instructors. Her mother enrolled her in piano lessons at the age of four, and she studiously applied herself to classical masterpieces for years before discovering punk music at the age of sixteen. Her extra-curriculars included student government (senior class president), marching band (drum major for two years), mathematics team (at her mother's request), robotics club (her choice), and the school paper (as the senior editor). As valedictorian, she gave the best speech at our graduation, and she readily procured a merit-based full-ride to virtually any college of her choosing, with test scores that were off the charts. It begged the question, then, what she was doing taking a seat next to me.

She hoisted her carry-on into the overhead compartment and flopped down beside me with a sigh. Ugh, she said, turning off her phone. Shouldn't've packed so much stuff.

I laughed. Not like we're travelling hundreds of miles to live on our own for the first time in our lives, or anything.

She punched my arm lightly. Touché, she said.

Our destination was Mebaun University, a smallish, relatively-unheard-of affair, not Ivy League or anything, as evidenced by the fact that they let my sorry ass in. What Alice saw in them was beyond me, and I worried that she was settling so that we wouldn't end up separated. Their computer-science division couldn't be that out of this world.

The flight attendants gave their usual opening babble, and I pretended to pay attention. Her hands finally free, Alice nudged me excitedly. You ready for this?

Yeah, I said as the attendants finished their spiel, ignoring the pain in my chest which told me otherwise. The airplane shifted under us as it got into runway position. You?

Alice laughed in her characteristic manner. Dude. I was born ready, she replied. I was talking to Miguel, and he was all idk how i feel abt u chicago man, what if i dont make any friends, and I was like, are u kiddin me rn u gon be the most popular dude there, and, like, IDK. I feel like there's this thing…

The plane started accelerating, and we were off the ground. Distracted, I closed the book that had been lying open in my lap, watching the ground fall away beneath us. The ivory clouds were soft and expansive as we rose into their midst, leaving the greyness of the city behind.

…and like packing my stuff for me, and it was like, Mom. I get that you wanna feel useful, but I got it. I'm in college now…

In college now. The phrase had a heavy weight to it, and I felt like it had yet to fully sink in. I looked around the cabin to keep my thoughts in warmer waters—it was pretty quiet, mostly suits with their faces in ledgers and screens and books. At the front, attendants were already beginning to pass out refreshments. Headphones abounded.

For her part, Alice was already pulling back out her phone. I don't even know, man, she said. How 'bout you?

I hadn't been paying attention, obviously. What do you mean? I asked.

Family giving you shit or anything?

Oh. Nah. It was true. I had half-expected my mother to fly up with me, but work obligations got in the way, apparently, and as sweet as she was there was no way my sister was making the trip on her own. They were pretty casual about seeing me leave, though—I think they were just happy that I got into somewhere relatively okay.

Lucky. Alice pulled her phone back out of her pocket and started to power it back on.

The refreshments finally reached our aisle, cutting our conversation short. I got a Coke—sweet, caffeinated beverages always helped me to deal with stress. The ice melted into my cup in that way that only airline ice can, and I held the flavour in my mouth for a moment before swallowing. My mouth felt dry, and I took another sip.

I tried to make progress in my book, but of course I couldn't concentrate, so I closed it and leand up against Alice's shoulder. She was messaging someone, now, and I could feel the screentaps resonating through her upper arm. I glanced over her body: She was taller than I was, and more fit, dammit—I closed my eyes. Stress never helped with these sorts of things.

I took a deep breath, and drifted off to sleep.

Sure thing, I said. She knew me too well. See you soon.

Her fingers ran through her hair. God, I hope not. It's one of the reasons I picked this place.

How so? She hadn't mentioned this to me before.

Diversity, y'know? You go to too pretentious a place, and it's like bam. Cookie-cutter level maximum. Only a certain kind of people going there, after all. And if you don't fit that mold, well… She shrugged. I don't know. It feels weird talking about it. It's not something I usually worry about.

I put my arm around her waist as we walked. Diffuse the tension. And here I thought you were only coming here for me, I told her.

She gave me a look. It evolved into an eye-roll. I can have multiple reasons, she said.

As if sensing the ebb in the conversation, her watch let off a soft chime. She glanced at the face, then stopped in her tracks. Oh shit, she said. I'm, uh, gonna have to bail. You think you can make it back on your own?

Um, yeah, of course. With food in my stomach, I actually felt pretty good. What's up?

Oh, I just have a meeting—sorry, man, I totally spaced it. Blah blah Mebaun University students diverse backgrounds free dessert—y'know. She gestured with one hand into the air. One of those things. I'll grab you a cookie, okay?

I wanted to be around for when Alice got done with her meeting, and I wasn't in the mood for unpacking, so I passed up the hallway to my room and headed instead for the floor's common area. It was empty, save for one girl sitting at the table, nose in a book. She wore a leather jacket and gloves, black jeans, a loud t-shirt for a band I'd never heard of. Her hair was chopped short and dyed black; her lips were pursed as her eyes traced the page.

I wondered if I should leave. A large part of me didn't want to. I glanced at my watch, staring at the face but not bothering to read the time. Fuck it, I thought. It's my first day.

She looked friendly. I took a seat to her right.

05 Conversation

The girl didn't notice my presence at first. I immediately questioned the wisdom of my decision, although it was now too late to turn back—I wasn't about to get up and leave after having just sat myself down. I tried words instead: Um. Hi?

She looked up and finally acknowledged my existence. Oh, she said. Hi. The room froze for a moment in silence.

It was more words than I had said in one breath for probably over a year, but to my surprise the girl seemed to soften a little upon hearing them. Her book closed slowly between her hands. I'm Rachel, she said.

Rachel. Somehow, those two words seemed more profound than all of mine put together. I was at a loss for words. I, uh… I'm sorry, I'm not very good at this.

06 Closing Statements
