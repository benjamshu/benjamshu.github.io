What follows is a theoretical foray into the Internet, its structures, and the implications thereof for the queer bodies caught up within its framework.Although some manifestations of gayness certainly are queer, my emphasis in this paper are on those identities which are more difficult to make legible under the data networks of the online world: people who are ace, pan, poly, trans, intersex, genderqueer, et cetera.

Because this is academia, and I want to matter, I'm tempted to lead off with some remark about how this sort of thing has never been attempted before, and thus position myself as a pioneer in a new and emerging field. But pioneers are old-school, and casting myself as the hero doesn't really do anyone else any favours. I certainly hope that I'm not the first one to talk about this stuff, in any case.

Nevertheless, it seems important to me to provide in this Preface some legitimization of my work, if only to give readers a good sense of its strengths and weaknesses from the start. And so, I find myself turning to an essay by Sara Ahmed, ‘Queer Feelings’:

To be comfortable is to be so at ease with one's environment that it is hard to distinguish where one's body ends and the world begins. One fits, and by fitting, the surfaces of bodies disappear from view. The disappearance of the surface is instructive: in feelings of comfort, bodies extend into spaces, and spaces extend into bodies. The sinking feeling involves a seamless space, or a space where you can't see the 'stitches' between bodies.

Sara Ahmed, ‘Queer Feelings’, in The Cultural Politics of Emotion, second edition (New York: Routledge, 2004), 148.

As Ahmed remarks in her essay, comfort often makes the boundaries of a space invisible, and its brushstrokes appear natural. When we are comfortable, the bodies and spaces we inhabit expand, and their forms become difficult to decipher. Discomfort, on the other hand, brings things into a sharp, often painful, focus, as one's awareness of both body and space are made impossible to ignore. If we are going to speak critically, then, we must speak from a place of discomfort.

So if my writing this needs justification, let it be this: I have seen the Internet, and it discomforts me.

More concretely: The past few years have seen a staggering increase with respect to the regulation, demarcation, and targeted attack of queer and racialized bodies, in a manner as yet unprecedented in spaces online. This is not to say that queer bodies have not been regulated or subject to attack in digital spaces before now—they have. But as the structures and forms of digital space have solidified, the mechanisms by which normativity is enforced have become themselves normalized and more deeply entrenched within the systems in which they operate, reinforced and made more potent by the concurrent expansion of the possibility for surveillance and of avenues for violence through digital means. We are all of us scared. And while the Internet has radically transformed us, opening up new avenues for connection, support, collaboration, and self-care, creating new modes of agency and expression with respect to identity, community, and desire, so too does it leave much to be sought after in its pursuit of becoming a liveable space. Real structural change is needed, and such change must take root at the level of proliferating conversations and understanding. Consider this my humble contribution to that endeavour.

What follows is also my undergraduate thesis in the field of Gender Studies.

This means a couple of things: One, that I researched and wrote it in, like, a month, which means that my potential for being thorough is somewhat diminished; two, that it was written for a grade and in an academic context; three, that I probably don't actually know what I'm talking about. As I will remark further in my Introduction, several journalists, organizations, and publications have been on the scene for years, and as a relative newcomer to the conversation, I, myself, have a lot to learn. But while I acknolwedge my own limitations as an author and theorist, I am not about to sit idly by as violence continues to be enacted on the people, organizations, and movements that I care about. We all need to be having this conversation more often. This thesis is but a part.

The internet has changed us. Email, then instant messaging, then social media, have vastly refigured our understanding of connectivity, social networks, and community, as emoji, kitty pidgin, and tweetspeek have revolutionized our approach to the written word. Blogging, hypertext, and wiki technologies have changed the face not only of journalism but information technologies in general, with networks of texts expanding ever outwards in a new and dynamic conversation. Digitial access and distribution has redefined accessibility not only to works of media, but to the tools and pathways for creating and re-producing culture on our own (slashfic and GIF culture, here's looking at you).

Meanwhile, the word accountability found itself imbued with new meaning, as everybody from the largest corporation to the smallest journalist has found themselves suddenly approachable by any and every member of their audience. Organized hashtag campaigns have been leveraged both as mechanisms for speaking out against injustice and as tools for brute-force harrassment, sometimes simultaneously, as one group of individuals co-opts the project of another. Online petitioning systems have made it easier than ever to show solidarity, while critiques of slacktivism and liberal showmanship have called into question what solidarity even means. And all the while, millions of people find themselves managing their work relationships, checking with friends, purchasing music, streaming videos, and tweeting to nobody and everybody about their lives, the massive cultural and political shifts happening around them little more than a subtle undercurrent in their day-to-day lives.

Of course, for those who have been living through these changes, none of this is news. People have been writing about their experiences online for as long as the medium has provided them with an opportunity to do so, and in recent times the extent of this journalism has only increased. Medium, a publishing platform created by Twitter co-founder Ev Williams, has become home to a variety of innovative publications pushing the boundaries of critique, from Matter to Points to THOSE PEOPLE to The Message—and, of course, longstanding publications like Model View Culture have been on the scene for years. Documentary projects like strolling and #WhitePeople, video essayists like Feminist Frequency, podcasts like Intersection, blogs like feministkilljoys… these all represent the new face of journalism online.

This paper is greatly indebted to the work of these individuals, and I'll be speaking more directly in conversation with many of them in the chapters to come. In short, the question I pose is this: How are digital spaces constructed, and what are the implications of this on mechanisms of power and identity online? More importantly, how can we negotiate these factors to broaden the scope of livable lives past that of patriarchal, white-supremacist normativity?

In this Introduction, I have done my best to frame the conversation that follows. Chapter Two will continue my line of inquiry into the idea of the internet as space—or rather, spaces. How do internet systems and structures come to be experienced as spaces, and how are these spaces formed, both in relation to each other, and to the bodies which inhabit them? For that matter, how are bodies formed online, and how do bodies take up space? How are boundaries created and inforced in digital spaces, both implicitly and explicitly, and what comes to denote a queer space? A safe space? A livable space?

In Chapter Three, our understanding of space becomes more complicated, as we examine the ways in which internet spaces are by nature mediated. How do the affordances provided by digital spaces come to structure the cultures which develop inside them, and how does culture at-large shape the affordances which are deemed important to provide? How have specific digital spaces dealt with mediation in the past, and what can this teach us about their nature?

Finally, Chapter Four ties these themes back into concepts of identity, visibility, and individual action. How are identities constructed online, and to what extent are these constructions forced onto their keepers? How are identities made visible and/or invisible by spaces and mediated structures, and how does anonymity intersect with indentity performance? How are identities created through data, and how do they exceed the data which defines them?

The scope of these questions are enormous, and this paper can provide no definitive answers. But at their heart is a deeper, more essential focus: What can we do to make sure we can pass through these spaces and come out alright? Not good, not even safe—not yet. But alright. It is not a question with an easy answer, and the conversation is ongoing.
