The story of the American social justice movements is the story of the transition between the naturalization of class elitism, white supremacy, patriarchy, and heteronormativityAnd soon, cisnormativity. and their formulation as extinct. Born in a time after the supposed advancements of the New Deal, the Civil Rights Movement, First- and Second-Wave Feminism, and now Marriage Equality, Millennials and Post-Millennials have been raised to operate under the assumption that equality has been achieved and the age of social justice is over.

From this starting point, there are two possible ways for one's worldview to evolve. For some, generally well-off cis white men, often in STEM-like sectors which emphasize measurable, objective truth, the best course of action is to double-down on this fantasy, dismissing, debating, or attacking any evidence to the contrary. Because the dominant Millennial narrative views obstacles to social justice as localized evils which must be combatted and contained, any recognition of these problems in one's own actions becomes a threat that must be guarded against. By necessity, society is conceived as a meritocratic utopia, in which those with the skills to succeed always will—for those in positions of success, this becomes a source of validation, and for those willing to recognize imbalances on the axes of race and gender, this can lead to the conclusion that white men consistently find themselves in positions of privilege for the sole reason that they are actually literally better than everyone else.

A great deal has been written on the nature and implications of this mentality (see Appendix A), so I'm not going to go into it here. Instead, I want to focus on everyone else; that is, those people who have been sold an image of reality that is free and equal, but then later realized, for whatever reason, that that is not this world. I'm going to call this outlook Post-Millennial, in that it is a radical troubling of the Millennial outlook which preceeds it, and I would argue that the distinction between these two ways of looking at the world has supplanted traditional formulations like conservative and liberal to form the foundation for the modern American political axis. In a generation raised on the belief that all people are created equal, that the institutions of capitalism and meritocracy are fair and balanced, that the American state is exceptional and justified, and that conditions of inequality exist firmly in the past, the divide between Millennial and Post- lies precisely between those committed to the fantasy, and those vehemently critical of it.

Before looking at the ways our understanding of gender is being conceptualized and reworked by Millennial and Post-Millennial sensibilities, we first need to develop our understanding of what gender is. I will do so by building upon and critiquing existing work in feminist and queer theoretical fields, moving away from a simple binary- or spectrum-based model to one built on relationality and assemblage, before demonstrating how, when viewed through a dyadicocispatriarchial lens, this consolidates itself into the formal conceptualizations of masculinity and femininity we recognize today.

As I mentioned in my response to Gaga Feminism, I'm not interested in a model of gender which depends upon biology, sexuality, or reproduction; the presence of trans ace individuals demonstrates that gender must operate even when removed from these contexts. However, the fact cannot be overlooked that these factors do play a role in how gender functions—for some individuals if not for all. In Queer and Now, Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick provides a mechanismEve Kosofsky Sedgwick, Queer and Now, in The Routledge Queer Studies Reader, edited by Donald E. Hall and Annamarie Jagose with Andrea Bebell and Susan Potter (New York: Routledge, 2013), 7‚Äì9. for handling this disconnect: by viewing gender (in her words, sexual identity) as not a simple masculine/feminine binary, but rather as the confluence of a wide variety of factors, some of which resonate with one another, others of which might not. To Sedgwick, this is the (or, at least, a) very definition of queerness: the open mesh of possibilities, gaps, overlaps, dissonances and resonances, lapses and excesses of meaning when the constituent elements of anyone's gender, of anyone's sexuality aren't made (or can't be made) to signify monolithically.

As Figure 1 shows, however, approaching gender from the perspective of a list of attributes, the values of which may or may not align according to normative standards, risks reducing it into a simple protocol to which gendered individuals might be said to conform. Such an approach is of only limited use, as it neglects to describe in any meaningful way the modes by which these attributes inform and are informed by each other; furthermore, its object-oriented emphasisFor more on the dangers of an object-oriented approach, see my thesis proposal and my response to Jasbir Puar. makes the assumption that these attributes have meaning in isolation, that one can be said to have a sex, or a gender, without reference to another.

However, we can make a more nuanced application of Sedgwick's formulation, conceiving of gender not as a protocol but rather as a general modality determined by a variety of attributes, the number, quality, and values of which are formed not statically but instead through relation to others. As I will discuss in ¬ß03, the existence of gender is necessitated by our position within a patriarchial structure. However, the forms that this gender can take, the ways it is displayed and interpreted, its number and fluidity, its legibilty and acceptability, and its overall impact on a person's life can vary widely. As such, it would be foolish to think of gender as a binary, as a set of two elements (even considering the possibility of embodying both or neither). In many cases, the only apparent tie between models of masculinity and femininity are their levels of acceptability for male-bodied and female-bodied individuals, respectively; as we have already noted, however, privileging sex as the defining factor in gender formulation ignores the incredible amount of diversity that gendered forms can take.

In The Legend of Korra, an animated television series by Bryan Konietzko and Michael Dante DiMartino, we can look to the primary cast for an example of how gender diversity can take shape. Korra, the series' protagonist, is headstrong, rugged, independent, emotional, impatient; she cares about living up to others' expectations for her and performing her duty to the world. She pays little attention to her appearance, never wears makeup, and in the fourth season, cuts her hair short. Asami, her eventual girlfriend, is flexible, composed, reliant, thoughtful, calculating; she cares about salvaging her family's legacy and being a source of support for those around her. She is always seen in lipstick and eyeshadow, her clothes are usually the height of fashion, and her hair is long and flowing. However, despite the immediate differences in gender presentation, both characters are depicted as equally valid representations of femininity, and never in the show is either suggested to be either less competent or less womanly because of it.

Such a hypothetical gender-state takes its shape only through contact with its neighbours, and we can imagine a history of growth and decay, conquest and defeat, colonization, revolution, and independence, through which our current landscape has formed.

It is important to note that the model of gender which I have proposed is not of an inherently problematic nature—in a world in which citizenship within and movement between gender-states is not regulated by power structures, in which the boundaries and conditions of states are in constant flux and open to change, in which new ones may crop up at any time and old ones may fade away, and in which legibility and grievability is made available to all, gender poses no unique threat to identity or personhood. This is, of course, not the world in which we live. But I do want to stress the fact that gender itself does not need to be undone; rather, the conditions which structure and mediate its boundaries need to be evaluated and brought into question.

The most well-known and theorized of these structures is that of the patriarchy, which predicates itself on the assumption (01) that genders are categorizable into at least two distinct groups, and (02) that one of these groups (masculinity) is more desirable than the others. Patriarchy itself does not foreclose the opportunity for more than two gender categories; as we have seen with white supremacy, it is possible to have a wide variety of categorizations without threatening the sovereignty of the dominant form. The gender binary as we understand it today depends instead upon dyadiconormativity,< dyadic ("male/female") + normativity the assumption that sex is an unambiguous biological determination of exactly one of {male, female}, and cisnormativity, the assumption that sex and gender must correlate in a meaningful and societally predetermined way. When taken together, dyadicocisnormatity imposes onto individuals societal categories of masculinity and femininity based on a premise of assumed biological cohesion. This combines with patriarchial normativity to function as a privileging of certain individuals over others, predecated on conformance to structural norms.

Because dyadicocisnormativity posits that (01) every individual has a sex, male xor female, and (02) that sex and gender are linked, every individual operating under a dyadicocisnormative structure finds themselves interpellated necessarily as either masculine or feminine. This is reflected in the English third-person pronouns, he and she, and the fact that our nongendered pronoun, they, is also the one we use for underpants. Dyadicocisnormativity tolerates androgyny only insofar as it is, at best, ambiguously human, or at least ambiguously defined.

Dyadicocisnormativity also functions to produce transness, firstly by supposing that individuals whose gender does not match the cisdyadic expectation must instead be attempting its alternative, and secondly by imposing the requirement that if an individual's sex and gender don't match in the dyadicocisnormatively prescribed way, one or the other must be brought into line. Transnormative expectations that non–gender-conforming individuals must desire surgury or at least hormones perform a re-situating of queer bodies into dyadicocisnormative prescibed lines by allowing the mutability of sex insofar as such changes contribute to the normative cohesion of the whole.

Under the Millennial fantasy, the patriarchy is considered extinct, and dyadicocisnormativity is often illegible. As such, it is easy to view gender as a vestige of the past—more often than not, this ends up naturalizing masculinity while targeting femininity as excessive, irrational, or unneccessary. As Millennials come to recognize the extent to which gender still operates in modern society, however—getting woke, either through deep conversations, sustained microaggressions, or traumatic events like #GamerGate—they become more and more aware of the extent to which their bodies are interpellated as gendered subjects. This contrast—between thinking of oneself as a nongendered subject in a nongendered world and realising the profound extent to which gender shapes our daily lives—can be traumatic for individuals whose interpellated form differs significantly from their initial viewing of themselves.

However, Millenials are provided with a surprising amount of agency in shaping the way in which their bodies are perceived and interpellated—and they're damn good at it, too. As the musical artist Halsey recently commented in an interview, We've created a generation that knows how to brand itself. Think about social media, usernames, profile pictures, the tweets you're putting out—you are branding yourself‚Ä¶ The same website where a mom is posting a picture of her children at the beach because she wants to share it with her family—on that same website someone else is making $100,000 off of a sponsored post because they've garnered so much attention. Especially in online spaces, where the visibility of dyadic sex is often optional, Millenials who have identified the mechanisms of interpellation are often able to twist them to their advantage, challenging cisdyadic and patriarchical norms.
