As a part of pre-registration this past year, Whitman College students were asked for their preferred name and pronouns, a change set in motion by Resolution SRS15.5, passed by the Associated Students of Whitman College at the end of last year. Although I have mixed feelings about the ways in which pronoun selection forces queer-identified individuals to situate themselves within a dyadicocisnormative rhetorical binary (and quantify themselves as data bodiesSee Jasbir Puar, Terrorist Assemblages), being able to choose the manner in which you are represented and made visible is important, and I think that this move is a step forward.

Book review

I read a lot of books for my thesis, and the latest, It's Complicated: The Social Lives of Networked Teens, is a gem. It's targeted at an adult demographic, which means sometimes you might find yourself rolling your eyes and saying well, duh, but it takes on the dynamics of teen life in a digital age surprisingly well. From a theoretical standpoint, its conceptualization of social media as an interconnected structure of networked publics is super useful, and its distinction between "being public" and "being in public" is a crucial one. Privacy becomes less something you have than something you do, in a landscape where surveillance is everywhere and visibility is only sometimes opt-out (and rarely, if ever, opt-in).
