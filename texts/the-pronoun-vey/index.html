let's talk about gendre

Due to the eroticization of the word sex in the twentieth century and the cultural decision to use gendre as a replacement, we generally think of grammatical gendre in terms of masculine versus feminine, and yeah, this is how some languages treat the concept. However, in many proto–indo-european languages, there are actually three gendres: masculine, feminine, and what is traditionally called neuter but often is better described as inanimate. We can see this distinction in our third-person singular personal pronouns: he, she, and it.

Given the modern connotations and understandings of the word gendre, it is tempting to think of it as a means of classification, but when talking about grammatical gendre (which pre-dates the more modern concept), it is important to recognize that this function is only secondary. Some languages do have features with this function; they are referred to as noun classes or the aptly-named classifiers

What sets grammatical gendre apart from these sorts of features, though, is the matter of agreement. Strictly defined, grammatical gendre is used to link constituents, either determiners and adjectives to nouns (compare spanish un libro and una llave) or pronouns to antecedents. In descendents of protoâ€“indo-european, gendre serves less as a means of putting words into boxes (categorize), than as a way of marking links between words. This explains is why many languages assign grammatical gendre to objects with no inherent cultural gendre traits. The femininity of llave, in spanish, is as a result of the fact that it is modified by feminine-marked adjectives, not the other way around.

This, of course, begs the question as to why gendre has such strong connotations if it is simply a grammatical word-linking process, and one explanation is that the associated meanings of gendre emerged as a sort of memory-aid. To use our previous example in spanish, remembering that llave goes with -a can be somewhat tedious; on the other hand, associating llave (key) with femininity is relatively easy. Then, if we can just recall that all feminine things (with a few exceptions, perhaps) use -a endings, we're set. And so grammatical gendre becomes gendred, so to speak.

The point is, grammatical gendre does serve a purpose: It clarifies the link between constituents. To demonstrate this, consider the following sentence in spanish: Saca el libro y la llave; ponlo en la mesa y ponla en la mochila (Get the book and the key; put it on the table and put it in the backpack). In english, it is impossible to know what each it in the sentence refers to, but in spanish, the difference between ponlo and ponla clarifies that the first refers to the book, and the second to the key.

Here's the catch, though: Grammatical gendre may serve a purpose, but in english, gendre isn't grammatical. There are no formal grammatical rules which state when you must use he, she, or it; the difference between these words is lexical, in their meanings, not in where you can use them. As an example, referring to a person with it isn't ungrammatical, it's just, well, offensive.

As perhaps another way of looking at things, consider the english honorifics miss and mrs. There are lexical rules for when you should use one or the other, certainly, but to think of miss and mrs as belonging to different gendres is silly. You will note that this binary already has an invented ambiguous third-form—and he and she relate in exactly the same way.

so gendre isn't grammatical, so what?

This is important because it makes the process of creating a nongendred pronoun remarkably easy. In french or spanish, removing gendre from the language is an impossible task, but in english, it's completely reasonable. This isn't a matter of restructuring our grammars, it's a case of there-isn't-a-word-for-this-thing-i-want-to-express. Fortunately, the english language is very well suited to handle this sort of situation: If there isn't a word for what we want to express, we just make one up.

And yet. To date, no nongendred pronoun has emerged with wide acceptance. There is certainly need—legal texts and academic papers are chock-full of unweildy he-or-shes; gendre theorists curse the binary that these two words create—and there have certainly been attempts, but so far none have stuck on a large scale. And maybe this is not a bad thing—we certainly don't all have to use the same nongendred pronoun, this is english, for crying out loud—but the hope is that we all have some option that we feel comfortable using.

This proposal exists because i was not satisfied with the preexisting pronoun options available to me. It certainly has its strengths and weaknesses, and i am not about to claim that the pronoun that someone else is using is wrong or that mine is objectively better or whatever. It is better-suited for me, but that's not the same as better-suited for everyone.

vey: the pronoun

This proposal exists to establish vey as a nongendred pronoun for use in the english language. The pronoun comes in a variety of forms, as follows: vey (nominative), ve (objective), vy (possessive determiner), vyn (possessive pronoun), vyself (reflexive). Its design features the following strengths:

  • The declension vey/ve/vy/vyn phonetically mirrors the declension i/me/my/mine and thou/thee/thy/thine
  • The lemma (nominative) form, vey, is phonetically similar to that of the third-person plural pronoun, they, but distinct enough to avoid confusion
  • The inital v sound of vey occurs in the same region of mouth as the other personal pronouns: me, we, thou, they
  • Vey is not in any way secondary to or derived from the existing he/she binary. It exists not as an alternative to he or she but as a replacement

The big problem, in my mind, with most invented nongendred pronouns is that they tend to try to mirror he and she, the very pronouns that they seek to replace. Why not, instead, model them after the pronouns that we intend to keep? Vey takes this approach, departing fully from the gendre binary while maintaining a diverse yet familiar—and easy-to-remember—set of forms.
